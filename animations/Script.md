| 02 First, a refresher on the rules of Yahtzee.  You have 5 dice, which you have to roll in various combinations to fill in 13 spots on the scorecard. Each box has to be filled in exactly once during the game, so every game of Yahtzee lasts 13 rounds. Each round, you roll all 5 dice.  Once you do that, you can choose some dice to keep and reroll the rest.  Then you can reroll a second time, again choosing whichever dice you want to keep and reroll.  Once you’ve finished 3 rolls, you have to fill in one of the empty boxes on the scorecard with what you have.   You can fill in a box for something you don’t have on your dice, but then you have to put a 0\. The 13 boxes on the card work as follows.  The top 6 boxes are for ones through sixes.   You get as many of a particular number as you can, and then you add up the dice of just that number to get points for that box.   There’s also a bonus for the top section.  If you get at least 63 points in the numbered boxes, then you get a 35 point bonus added to your score. The bottom section has a bunch of specialized combinations.  There’s 3 of a kind and 4 of a kind, where you need, well 3 or 4 of a kind.  If you get one of those, you get points equal to the sum of all of the dice. Next is full house.  Like a full house in poker, you need 3 of one number and 2 of another.  This gives you 25 points. Then we have small straight (4 numbers in a row), worth 30 points, and large straight (5 numbers in a row) worth 40 points. Finally, there’s the big one, the Yahtzee.  If you get 5 of the same number, you get 50 points. There’s also a chance box at the bottom.  You can fill this in with the sum of your dice, regardless of what’s on them. In addition to being the most valuable turn in the game, the Yahtzee is also special for another reason.  It’s the only thing you’re allowed to get multiple times.  If you get a yahtzee after the yahtzee box is already filled in, you get to fill in a different box according to some weird rules we won’t get into. If you fill in the full house, small straight, or large straight using these rules, you get the full points, even though your 5 of a kind wouldn’t normally qualify. If you filled in the yahtzee box with a 50 and not a 0, then you get a 100 point bonus for every additional yahtzee you roll. |  Bring in dice from bottom, scorecard from left Highlight boxes one at a time Divide right into 3 sections. | 03 To understand Yahtzee, we first have to understand dice.  When you roll 5 dice, the number of outcomes is 6^5, or 7776, but the number of *different* outcomes is a lot less, only 252, since we only care about how many of each number show up, and not which die gives which number. That doesn’t mean that each of the 252 outcomes is equally likely.  For cases with all dice the same (or, just a yahtzee), there is only one way for that outcome to occur. For cases with all dice different (including both large straights), there are a lot more ways: 120 to be exact. This means that you are 40 times more likely to get a large straight on your first roll than you are to get a Yahtzee, even though there are 6 ways to get a Yahtzee, but only 2 ways to get a large straight. Other combinations of dice can have different frequencies. Rerolls definitely change the math around which of these outcomes are most likely, but the game’s strategy is going to depend a lot on which of these outcomes can be achieved in the most ways. |  Show animation of 6^5 with dots. | 04 So here’s how we’re going to master this game.  We’ll use a computer science technique called dynamic programming to figure out the optimal strategy for every possible position you could find yourself in in a game of Yahtzee.  We’ll first pretend that you’re playing by yourself, so we’re just trying to get as many points as we can on average.  We’ll talk about multiplayer games later.  If you’re worried that you accidentally clicked on a video trying to teach computers to play yahtzee, don’t worry.  This is just what we need to do to figure out good strategies for humans to use. So here’s the plan.  We start at the end of the game and work backwards.  After your last roll on your last turn, you just fill in whichever box is open and you get some points for it. So what do we do on the second reroll of our last turn?  Well, once we choose which dice to roll, we can figure out the chances of every possible outcome after the reroll.  We take these probabilities, multiply by the points we get in each case, and add the results together to get the average number of points we’ll get if we reroll those specific dice. We can do this for every possible group of dice to reroll, and whichever gives us the biggest number is the group that we should reroll.  And if you’re not an expert at calculating probabilities in your head, then again, that’s mostly what the computer is for. Once we’ve done this for every possible position we could be in before the second reroll, we can go back a step to the first reroll.  For every possible roll of the dice, we see how many points we get, but instead of using the final points, we use the *average* points that we just calculated from our second reroll.  We again choose the dice to reroll to maximize the average points, and we keep track of what that average is. We can then average over all possible first rolls to get the expected score before the beginning of the last turn From there, we can keep going back to the previous turn.  Now, we might have a decision to make at the end of the turn for which box to fill in, but we make that decision the same way as before.   We get the average points for each possible decision (in this case, that’s the points we get for this turn plus the average points starting next turn) and make the decision with the highest average. We then do the same thing for the second reroll of turn 12, then the first reroll of turn 12, then go back to turn 11 and keep going. At every step, we get the average points until the end of the game for every possible position that we can be in, so that we’re ready to do the calculations when we have to calculate the previous step.  Once we get back to the beginning of the game, we’ve calculated a full strategy, and also an important number: the average number of points for a whole game under perfect play. |  Bring in empty scorecard Bring in dice to top position. | 06 With that in mind, let’s start at the end.  On the last turn of the game, we’ll be going all out to try to fill in one specific box.  Let’s figure out what we need to do for each one, starting with the easiest cases.  We’re going to ignore bonuses that you get for extra yahtzees and just focus on getting the thing in each box. If your last box is in the top section, the strategy is straightforward.  Keep every die with the number you need.  Reroll everything else. After 3 rolls, you’ll get about 2.1 of your given number on average.  Here’s how often you’ll get each number of your given value.  If you need 2, you’re in decent shape.  If you need 3 or more, you’ll need some luck. The strategy for getting a Yahtzee is pretty similar.  Just keep whichever number you have the most of and reroll the rest.  If there’s a tie, just choose one value to keep.  Notice that this might change from your first reroll to your second. Unfortunately, your chances of actually getting a Yahtzee are less than 5%, so hopefully you’re not counting on this to win. Now, you’d think that the next simplest cases are 3 and 4 of a kind, but they’re actually more complicated for weird reasons.  We’ll get to them later. Full house is pretty straightforward.  You should keep pretty much any groups of dice that you have.  If you get 2 pairs, 3 of a kind, or a single pair, you should keep those dice and reroll the rest.  If you get 4 of a kind, you should keep 3 of them and reroll the other 2 dice. The chance of successfully filling in the full house box on your last turn is about 37%. Getting a bit more complicated is the large straight.  There are two ways to get a large straight.  12345 or 23456\. Or, to put it another way, you need 2, 3, 4, 5, and either 1 or 6\.  So the strategy looks like this. If you roll a 2, 3, 4, or 5, then keep one of each. For 1 or 6, it’s a bit more complicated.  You should never keep more than one, but if it allows you to keep at least 4 dice, then you should keep a 1 or 6\. If you would be keeping 3 or fewer dice, then you’re better off rerolling all of your 1s and 6s, because the increase in combinations helps more than keeping the extra die. Doing this strategy will get you the large straight box about 27% of the time. The small straight adds more complexity.  There are now 3 ways to get it: 1234, 2345, and 3456, plus there’s an extra die that doesn’t get used adding to the number of combinations. You still always need a 3 and a 4, but the other dice are more complicated. You should reroll everything if you don’t have a 3 or a 4\. If you do have 3s or 4s, you should keep one of each, as well as one each of your 2s and 5s if you have any. You should keep a 1 if you have a 1 and 2 but no 5, and you should keep a 6 if you have a 5 and 6 but no 2\. Your chances of filling the small straight box if you do this are pretty good: 62%.  That’s why small straight is one of the better boxes to leave around until the end. The next one is fun from a math perspective: Chance.  Since we only care about getting the highest total possible, we can treat each die individually.  How do we decide which ones to reroll? In the spirit of doing things backwards, let’s do the second reroll first.  If you roll a die, the average value that you get is 3.5.  This helps us to think about what to roll on our second reroll. For each die above 3.5 (456), it would get lower on average if we rerolled it.  For each die below 3.5 (123) it would get higher on average if we rerolled it, so we should reroll the 123s and keep the 456s. So what do you get on average if you do this?  Well, there’s a ⅙ chance of getting a 6, 5, or 4, plus a ½ chance that you reroll, which gives you 3.5 on average.  Averaging all of these out gives 4.25.  Why do we care about this number?  Well, we need it to figure out what to do on our first reroll. After one roll, we know that any die we reroll will have 2 chances to give us a high number.  This means that any die we reroll will now give us that 4.25 on average instead of just 3.5. This means that we now want to reroll the 4 as well as the 123, and keep the 56\. So some complicated math gives us a simple strategy.  Keep 56s on your first reroll, and 456s on your second. Doing it this way gives you an average of 4 ⅔ per die, or 23 ⅓ total for your chance. That brings us to our most complicated cases: the 3 and 4 of a kind.  At first, they seem like they should be simple.  Just try for as many of a number as possible.  And if your only goal is to put *something* in those boxes, then it is that easy.  We do basically the same thing as if we’re trying for a yahtzee. But if we want to play perfectly, these boxes are weird.  The score for these boxes is the sum of the dice.  Because of that, they combine the all-or-nothing nature of full house, yahtzee and the straights, with the score maximizing of chance.  To get this right, we have to combine the two approaches in sometimes counterintuitive ways. Starting with the 4 of a kind, the last roll is mostly what you expect.  Keep whatever you have the most of. If you already have a 4 of a kind, then you reroll your last die if it’s 3 or less to try to get more points. (simplify these cases) The first roll, however, is a bit strange.  If you have 4 of a kind you should keep it (and reroll your other die if it’s less than 5), but otherwise, it depends a lot on which numbers you have the most of.  Because our points come from the sum of the dice, we care about the chance of success but also the value of those dice if we succeed.  Because of that, we sometimes want fewer larger dice over more smaller ones. If we have, for example, 3 1’s and 2 6’s, our chances of success will be highest from keeping the 1’s: \_\_% versus \_\_%. However, we will get significantly fewer points on average if we get 4 1’s compared to 4 6’s.  Because of this, the average number of points that we get overall is actually higher if we keep the 6’s. In fact, there are several cases where it’s best to choose fewer of a larger number instead of more of a smaller one. Even though we’re not quite trying to maximize our chances, we end up with a 28% chance of getting our 4 of a kind, with an average score of about 6 points. 3 of a kind is similar.  On the second reroll, just keep whatever you have most of, and if you already have of 3 of a kind, reroll the other 2 dice if they’re less than 4\. On the first roll, we again usually want to keep the number we have the most of, but there are a lot of exceptions.
